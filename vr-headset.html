physical product design

introduction

Group Members:

Donny Wu, Ridge Zhang, Andrea Ma.

This final project was made for the material in design course IAT 336. Working In a group of three, we designed, prototyped, and created the final VR headset within a time span of 2 months.

This course is focused on how materials behave, manufacturing techniques, product design. The final project is an example of that where we were given two randomly chosen materials we had to use to complete our prototype. In this case, plastic and wood. We also have to design for ergonomics, ease of use, and for a persona.

tools used

Sketching

  • Used for initial design and iteration

Autodesk Maya

  • Low poly 3d modelling
  • Used for secondary refinements and prototyping using laser cutters utilizing UV unfold and packing features
  • Also used to form the VR headset to a human head for a comfortable fit.

SolidWorks

  • Final 3D printing STL creation, here I added many alignment tabs, precise slot creation for hinges and lenses, and foam cutting pattern creation.

design context

Our headset will be used outdoors, during evenings with clear skies, since we plan for it to be used to help our persona stargaze. The headset will aid our persona in identifying different constellations, or show a clearer images of the stars without light pollution getting in the way. As such, since the headset is mostly used as an aid, it will be taken on and off fairly frequently so our persona can compare the view in the headset, with the actual view outdoors. For example, our user would be taking a walk outdoors during a camping trip, and he could take the headset with him during the walk to learn about the stars he can see during his walk. Heâ€™ll be keeping the headset on for the entire walk, so he can flip the headset up or down depending on if he wants to see the physical stars, or the virtual stars with all the information about them attached to it.

One of the constraints we had while designing this VR headset is that we were constrained to using two different materials, wood and plastic, at roughly a 50/50 ratio on the exterior. This restriction makes it not feasible to create compound curves. When I was sketching, I kept the tools available to us in mind, which is a laser cutter and a 3d printer.

first iteration

I started with sketching out different forms that I thought would look cool and fit the persona of our project. With many to choose from, I chose one that could be good for a sketch model and went on making it viable for manufacture with the tools that we have. The one I've chosen have plenty of curves that might cause problems when making the sketch model, so I went on Maya to explore possibilities at the low poly style and possible flat packing solutions for the sketch model. I also imported a 3d head model to form the initial face device interface. One problem I faced when exporting the 3d model for laser cutting was how to export the UV unwrap file to a vector based file format. After a couple hours of research, I've found a script that can set each vertex's coordinates to UV coordinates, creating a flat plain model in the 3d view lower that can be rendered by the Maya's vector renderer.

second iteration

When making the second iteration of the physical prototype, I updated the Maya model so it looks better when sized to fit the actual VR headset. Having a higher polygon model, but only in one axis, which allows this design to still be flattened and laser cut. A thing I thought about was how can the headset be constructed so it can fit around the face nicely. I found that there are 3d scanned focal models online that I can scale to 90th percentile head size to use as a reference for the VR headset. The second sketch model is made out of cardstock since it is easy to laser cut, folds right on the dashed lines, and won't buckle at non-perforated lines. This helps in the folding and gluing process. A problem we faced was accounting in for the paper thickness because we did not know what kind of cardstock we were going to use to cut. So, when assembling, we needed to put a lot of effort stretching and glueing the top part to complete the shell.

final iteration

In the final model, I exported my slightly modified second Maya model into SolidWorks, which I can then play connect the dots game and loft the surfaces out. Through adding thickness to these surfaces and multiple boolean operations, the base 3d model shell is formed. Since we need to use wood and plastic in roughly 50/50 bases on the outside and we have plenty of curves, we had to use extremely thin, laser cut wood. This means that wood cannot be a structural element of the headset. ABS 3d printed plastic shell was created using the solid works model that was split into 4 pieces to fit inside the 3d printer. Each piece have alignment pegs to help alignment when assembling and glueing. The laser-cut pattern on the thin plywood was created first using the un triangulated UV unwrap pattern from Maya, then for every single quad, we had to map a vector cut pattern onto it using a special plugin that allows for 4 corners free transform. This is to allow for the curve to create the double radius curve needed in this project.

reflection

During the course of this project, I've applied and extended a lot of what I have hearned in the School of Interactive Arts and Technology. With solid works, I have learned a full array of new features include boolean operations, derived parts, importing files from Maya and lofting. I have learnt what software is good for what task. For example, Maya is great with organic shapes and making the first two iterations, but does not do well with boolean operations and precise drafting goals. Solidworks, on the other hand, is not very flexible when it comes to creating organic forms. Manufacturing processes and tolerances are also something I have come away with. The laser beam has a width that needs to be accounted for minimum cut distance. 3D printers have support materials to worry about and the orientation of how it is printed effects the structural integrity in one direction. At the end at the final public showcase, many people were impressed with the final project and it fits perfectly around the professor's head. We received the highest mark in the class for this project.
